# California Wildfire Research — FireSight Demo Reference

Comprehensive research for the FireSight hackathon demo. Every statistic, name, date, and location below is sourced from web research conducted March 2026.

## 1. THE JANUARY 2025 LA FIRES — The Most Emotionally Raw

### Overview
- **Dates**: January 7-31, 2025
- **Total fires**: 14 destructive wildfires across LA metro and San Diego County
- **Deaths**: At least 29-31 people killed
- **Evacuations**: More than 200,000 people forced to flee
- **Structures destroyed**: More than 18,000 homes and structures
- **Acres burned**: 57,529 acres
- **Economic cost**: $76 billion to $250 billion (estimates vary widely)
- **Insured losses**: Up to $45 billion
- **Cause/conditions**: Drought, low humidity, vegetation buildup, hurricane-force Santa Ana winds reaching 100 mph

### The Palisades Fire (Pacific Palisades)
- **Started**: January 7, 2025
- **Growth**: From 20 acres to 200 acres in 20 MINUTES
- **Total size**: 37 square miles (23,713 acres)
- **Deaths**: 12 people
- **Structures destroyed**: 6,837 — most destructive fire in LA city history
- **Rank**: 10th deadliest, 3rd most destructive California wildfire ever
- **What was destroyed**: Almost every structure north of Sunset Boulevard burned. Most of downtown Pacific Palisades was leveled. The entire Pacific Palisades Bowl Mobile Estates neighborhood was destroyed.
- **Historic losses**: The 708 House (1949 Richard Neutra), 1952 Southdown Estates, a 1969 Conrad Buff/Donald Hensman house on PCH, Will Rogers' historic ranch house
- **Celebrity homes destroyed**: Adam Brody & Leighton Meester, Anna Faris, Paris Hilton, Jeff Bridges, Mel Gibson, Billy Crystal, Diane Warren, Miles Teller ($12.1M home), Cary Elwes
- **Containment**: Fully contained January 31, 2025

### The Eaton Fire (Altadena)
- **Started**: Evening of January 7, 2025, in Eaton Canyon in the San Gabriel Mountains
- **Total acres**: 14,021
- **Deaths**: 17-19 people
- **Structures destroyed**: 9,414 — second most destructive fire in California history (after 2018 Camp Fire)
- **Rank**: 5th deadliest California wildfire ever

### Altadena: A Racial Justice Catastrophe
- Altadena is a **historically Black community** in the LA region — a symbol of resilience and opportunity
- **61% of Black households** were within the fire perimeter vs. 50% of non-Black households
- **48% of Black households** destroyed or sustained major damage vs. 37% non-Black
- **All but 1 of the 19 confirmed dead** lived west of Lake Avenue — the predominantly Black neighborhood that received evacuation orders HOURS after east Altadena
- Authorities knew about fire spread into western Altadena for **4+ hours** before sending evacuation alerts
- Fires came within hundreds of feet of victims' homes before any alerts were sent
- The displacement threatens to **permanently erase Altadena's Black community** — rising property values and preexisting barriers to homeownership mean displaced Black families may never return
- Decades of segregation and redlining left Black households more physically vulnerable

### Evacuation Chaos — Palisades
- Pacific Palisades is boxed in by mountains to the east; all roads funnel onto Pacific Coast Highway
- When fire exploded, residents rushed to cars, roads instantly clogged
- **People abandoned cars and fled on foot** along Pacific Coast Highway
- Some residents **took refuge in the ocean** to escape advancing flames
- Police told drivers to abandon vehicles and run
- Survivor Kelsey Trainor: "It was all smoke around us, fire everywhere. People are just honking their horns... Gridlock — nowhere to go." Ash was pelting her face. Took over an hour to reach safety.
- More than 70,000 people evacuated; many on foot

### Water Supply Failure
- Fire hydrants in Pacific Palisades **ran dry**
- Three 1-million-gallon water storage tanks all emptied:
  - First tank dry by 5 PM Tuesday
  - Second tank dry by 9 PM Tuesday
  - Third tank dry early Wednesday morning
- Water demand hit **4x normal levels** for 15 straight hours
- Firefighters couldn't get water up the hill; pressure dropped at higher elevations
- Radio traffic: **"The hydrants up here are dead"**
- LADWP claims they never "ran out" of water — the tanks just couldn't refill fast enough

### Alert System Failures
- The evacuation alert process involved a **byzantine chain**: Fire Dept/Sheriff identifies area -> communicates to OEM member at incident command -> phone call/text to another OEM staffer at emergency operations center -> system update -> public alert
- Each alert took **20-30 minutes** to issue (down from 60 minutes previously, but still too slow)
- Some areas never received evacuation orders at all
- **17 deaths in Altadena occurred in a square-mile pocket that never received alerts**
- People **refused to leave** because they were "waiting for official evacuation orders that never came"
- Power outages and cell tower failures disrupted digital alerts
- Older populations lacked access to digital alerts and had mobility challenges
- First responders couldn't share info due to unreliable cellular connectivity, inconsistent reporting methods, and unconnected communication platforms

### LAFD Staffing Crisis
- Less than **1 firefighter per 1,000 residents** (vs. ~2 per 1,000 in Chicago, Dallas, Houston)
- **$17.6 million budget cut** in the two years before the fires
- Budget cuts eliminated civilian positions like mechanics, impacting apparatus repair
- Experienced firefighters retiring faster than the department can recruit
- Firefighters reported running out of hoses, masks, and water tanks during peak fire days
- LA fire chief had **warned about budget cuts hampering emergency response** one month before the fires

## 2. THE CAMP FIRE — PARADISE, 2018 (Most Emotionally Devastating)

### The Facts
- **Date**: November 8, 2018
- **Location**: Butte County, Northern California
- **Cause**: Failed Pacific Gas & Electric (PG&E) transmission line hardware in Feather River Canyon during strong katabatic winds
- **Deaths**: 85 people — deadliest California wildfire EVER
- **Displacement**: 50,000+ people
- **Structures destroyed**: 18,804 — 85% of the town's buildings
- **Acres burned**: 153,000 (61,900 hectares)
- **Damage cost**: $16.5 billion
- **Speed**: The fire consumed ALL of Paradise in just SIX HOURS
- **Alert system failure**: Fewer than 30% of Paradise residents had signed up for the CodeRed emergency alert system. Only 64% of calls went through; only a third were answered by a person; 36% (~5,400 numbers) never connected at all.

### Evacuation Nightmare
- All five egress routes out of Paradise were closed by fire at least once during evacuation
- By 8:30 AM, traffic throughout Paradise had significantly deteriorated
- Fire overtook traffic on Pentz Road and upper Skyway — **burnovers trapping evacuees**
- Routes that normally took 25 minutes took **several hours**
- At least **7 people died trapped in their vehicles** on Edgewood Road
- 30 spot fires broke out throughout Paradise from ember showers BEFORE the main fire arrived, forcing simultaneous total evacuation
- The city had **reduced a main road from 4 lanes to 2** six years earlier, citing pedestrian safety
- Cars were abandoned; people fled on foot

### Paradise Today
- Pre-fire population: ~26,000
- Post-fire: dropped to fewer than 5,000 (2020)
- By 2024: recovered to ~11,000 (less than half)
- 3,000+ single-family homes and 600+ multi-family units rebuilt
- Recovery challenged by elderly and low-income population unable to afford rebuilding
- The town is **still recovering 7+ years later**

## 3. BAY AREA SPECIFIC FIRE THREATS (Where the judges live)

### The 1991 Oakland Hills Firestorm (Tunnel Fire)
- **Date**: October 20, 1991
- **Location**: Oakland/Berkeley hills, East Bay
- **Deaths**: 25 (23 civilians, 1 police officer, 1 firefighter)
- **Injuries**: 150
- **Structures destroyed**: 2,843 single-family homes + 437 apartment/condo units = 3,280+ total
- **Acres**: 1,520
- **Evacuations**: ~10,000 people
- **Wind gusts**: Up to 65 mph
- **Response**: 400 engine companies, 1,500 personnel, 250 agencies
- **Key detail**: 11 victims died in traffic jams on Charing Cross Road while evacuating
- This happened in the BACKYARD of SF judges — Oakland/Berkeley hills are visible from San Francisco

### Tubbs Fire (2017) — Wine Country
- **Date**: October 8-31, 2017
- **Location**: Napa, Sonoma, and Lake counties (started near Calistoga)
- **Deaths**: At least 22 in Sonoma County
- **Structures destroyed**: 5,643 total (2,668 single-family homes, 209 multifamily, 190 mobile homes, 31 commercial)
- **Acres burned**: 34,000+
- **At the time**: Most destructive wildfire in California history
- **Santa Rosa destruction**: The fire traveled 12 nautical miles to Santa Rosa city limits in just 3 hours
- **Coffey Park**: Over 1,300 structures destroyed — an entire suburban neighborhood LEVELED
- **Wind speeds**: 70 mph (near hurricane strength)
- **Cause**: Originally blamed on PG&E, later determined to be caused by homeowner equipment
- **Proximity to SF**: Santa Rosa is ~55 miles north of San Francisco. Judges may know people affected.

### Glass Fire (2020) — Napa Valley Wine Country
- **Date**: September 27 - October 2020
- **Location**: Napa and Sonoma counties
- **Acres burned**: 67,000+
- **Structures destroyed**: 1,235 buildings, ~300 homes
- **Homes damaged**: 150+
- **Wineries affected**: 23 wineries destroyed or damaged, 31 wineries/restaurants/hotels/resorts total
- **Notable losses**:
  - Chateau Boswell Winery — completely destroyed
  - Restaurant at Meadowood (three-Michelin-starred) — completely destroyed
  - Castello di Amorosa — lost critical storage building with thousands of bottles
- **Harvest impact**: Up to 80% of Napa Valley's 2020 Cabernet Sauvignon grapes couldn't be made into wine due to smoke taint
- **Most destructive fire ever to hit Napa Valley**
- **Proximity to SF**: Napa Valley is ~50 miles northeast of San Francisco

### Current Bay Area Risk Zones
- **Sonoma County**: Ranked #1 for largest increase in wildfire risk to homes
- **Napa County**: Ranked #2
- **Marin County**: Ranked #3
- **East Bay hills**: Dublin, San Ramon, Blackhawk, Livermore, Walnut Creek — high risk
- **Peninsula**: Woodside, Portola Valley
- **Marin**: Ross Valley
- **Diablo Winds**: Occur May and October; in 75 years (1923-1998), 11 Diablo wind fires burned 9,840 acres, destroyed 3,542 homes, killed 26 people
- New fire hazard severity maps show dramatic increases in Sonoma, Napa, and around San Jose

## 4. OTHER DEVASTATING CALIFORNIA WILDFIRES (2020-2024)

### August Complex Fire (2020)
- **Largest recorded California wildfire**: 1,000,000+ acres
- Northern California

### Dixie Fire (2021)
- **Dates**: July 13 - October 25, 2021
- **Acres**: 963,309 — largest SINGLE-SOURCE wildfire in California history
- **Structures destroyed**: 1,329 (600+ residential)
- **Cost to suppress**: $637.4 million — most expensive firefighting effort in US history
- **Cause**: PG&E equipment (again)
- **Town of Greenville**: 75% of structures destroyed, including historic Gold Rush-era downtown. Took less than 30 minutes. Compared to "a huge tornado."

### Park Fire (2024)
- **Date**: July 24, 2024
- **Location**: Butte County (same county as Camp Fire) — started in Bidwell Park, Chico
- **Acres**: 429,603 — 4th largest in California history, largest single fire of 2024
- **Structures destroyed**: 709
- **Cause**: ARSON — Ronnie Dean Stout II pushed a burning car into a gully
- **Largest fire ever caused by arson in California**
- **Containment**: September 26, 2024

### Woolsey Fire (2018)
- **Date**: November 8-21, 2018 (same day as Camp Fire)
- **Location**: LA and Ventura counties, Malibu
- **Acres**: 96,949
- **Deaths**: 3
- **Structures destroyed**: 1,643
- **Evacuations**: 295,000 people
- **Celebrity homes destroyed**: Miley Cyrus, Gerard Butler, Neil Young, Robin Thicke, Camille Grammer, Kim Basinger

### Climate Change Acceleration
- **+172% increase** in burned areas from 1971 to 2021 due to human-caused climate change
- **+320% increase** from 1996 to 2021
- **5x increase** in summer burned areas in Northern/Central California (1996-2021 vs. 1971-1995)
- Fire season starts **6 to 46 days earlier** than it did in 1992
- Dry season has increased by **23 days** since the climate was 1.3C cooler
- **By 2031-2050**: Summer burn area could increase another 52% above current levels

### Economic Costs (Cumulative)
| Year | Cost |
|------|------|
| 2017 | $24 billion (Tubbs, Thomas fires) |
| 2018 | $22-27.7 billion (Camp, Woolsey fires) |
| 2020 | $5-16 billion |
| 2025 | $76-250 billion (LA fires alone) |

### Insurance Crisis
- **100,000+ Californians** have lost their insurance since 2019
- State Farm and Allstate **stopped writing new policies** in California (2023)
- FAIR Plan (insurer of last resort) enrollment **more than doubled** from 2020 to 2024 (~452,000 policies)
- FAIR Plan costs **2x average** homeowner insurance ($3,200 vs ~$1,600)
- In Pacific Palisades specifically: 1,400 of 9,000 homes on FAIR Plan in 2024 (up from ~300 in 2020 — **quadrupled**)
- **806,651 California homes are completely uninsured** (out of 7.6 million)

### Towns That No Longer Exist (or barely do)
- **Paradise** — 85% destroyed in 2018, still at less than half population 7 years later
- **Greenville** — 75% destroyed in 2021, historic Gold Rush town wiped off the map
- **Canyondam** — largely destroyed by Dixie Fire, 2021
- **Indian Falls** — largely destroyed by Dixie Fire, 2021

## 6. WHAT GOES WRONG IN WILDFIRE RESPONSE — What FireSight Solves

### Communication Failures
- First responders use **different radio frequencies, platforms, and reporting methods** — cannot communicate across agencies
- Unreliable cellular connectivity in fire zones
- No unified communication platform for incident commanders
- Cell towers fail due to power outages
- Information overload at dispatch and incident command during initial response

### Evacuation Bottlenecks
- Alert systems require **multi-step bureaucratic chains** (20-30 minutes per alert in LA 2025)
- Opt-in alert systems mean most residents never get warned (only 30% signed up in Paradise)
- Evacuation routes become **death traps** — limited roads, simultaneous evacuation, fire crossing routes
- No real-time traffic/route optimization for evacuation
- Vulnerable populations (elderly, disabled, non-English speakers) get left behind
- People wait for "official" orders that never come while fire approaches

### Resource Deployment Issues
- Severe understaffing (LAFD: <1 firefighter per 1,000 residents)
- Budget cuts reduce equipment, training, overtime, maintenance
- No real-time tracking of where resources are deployed
- Aviation (50% of suppression budgets) poorly optimized
- Mutual aid coordination between agencies is slow and fragmented
- Water supply infrastructure fails under extreme demand (4x normal in Palisades)

### Situational Awareness Gaps
- Incident commanders lack real-time 3D visualization of fire spread
- No integrated view combining fire data, weather, terrain, resource positions, evacuation status
- Satellite imagery is too slow and blocked by smoke/clouds
- AI fire spread prediction exists in research but is not operationally deployed
- Data from multiple sources (drones, satellites, ground crews, weather) is not integrated

### Technology Gaps That FireSight Addresses
- No AR/VR-based command center for incident commanders to "see" the whole fire in 3D
- No AI agent orchestration for simultaneous drone deployment, evacuation routing, resource allocation, and fire prediction
- No world-model-based terrain visualization for real-time tactical planning
- First responders need voice, situational awareness, and alerting tech that works in wildland-urban interface
- Current systems are 2D maps, radio calls, and phone trees — not spatial, not real-time, not AI-assisted
