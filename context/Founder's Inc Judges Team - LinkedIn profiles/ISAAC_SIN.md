🔥First Place in Hardware Hackathon🔥

The Challenge:
We were provided with motors, propellers, and a controller with a receiver. Our task? Create a drone that was lightweight, durable, and balanced, capable of maintaining controlled flight. 
Here’s how we made it happen:
Design & Prototyping: I spearheaded the chassis and body design using SolidWorks, ensuring a lightweight yet durable structure to hold the electronics while maintaining balanced weight distribution for increased flight. To maximize efficiency under the tight timeline, we employed rapid prototyping, designing multiple chassis variations simultaneously. We 3D-printed them together using a gyroid infill geometry to boost structural integrity, then tested to find the optimal design.
Electronics: We designed a circuit schematic to integrate the motors and microcontroller board, meticulously soldering and wiring everything for reliability. 
Software: Using the Betaflight configurator, we programmed the controller-transmitter link, ensuring the motors responded precisely to inputs—moving in sync or opposition as needed.
Assembly: Finally, we mounted the electronics and battery onto the chassis, connected the drone to the controller, and achieved stable, controlled flight! 🛸
	
Reflection:
Our first place win at the drone hackathon was a whirlwind of action. With just 8 hours, we split up into two teams to increase efficiency, separating into electrical and mechanical teams. Hourly huddles kept everyone in sync, ensuring no one drifted off course as we bounced ideas off each other with constant updates. The clock was relentless, but we thrived in the heat of it, laser-focused as the 8 hours faded into minutes left. During soldering, we accidentally fried the RC receiver pin by soldering directly onto the pad. With no spare flight controllers available, I thought my first hackathon was over as quickly as it had begun, as there was no way to connect the RC. However, we didn’t give up, and sought all solutions. By checking the board’s documentation, we discovered another pin could be reconfigured as a receiver pin. This discovery saved our project, leading to our victory. That drone didn’t just fly, it was a triumph of our collective hustle, adaptability, and grit. 🏆

It was an unforgettable experience collaborating with Isaac Sin, Jared Shi, and Jason Lin to leverage our diverse skill sets and innovate under pressure. I’m incredibly proud of our team’s dedication and synergy. 

A massive Thank You to BU AIAA and BU Hackhardware for hosting this exhilarating event and providing a platform to push our limits. Great job to all the other teams as well who demonstrated their determination and creativity. 

I’d love to build upon this experience and connect with other passionate individuals in designing robotics, autonomous systems, and more.

Integrating CAN Bus to Blue Robotics Thruster by custom PCB and protocol

ResearchGate · May 1, 2021

Show publication
CAN (Controller Area Network) is a messaging protocol that allows various devices to communicate with eachother within the same bus network. This project intends to integrate CAN bus communication electronics with a Blue Robotics thruster. All electronics are required to behoused inside a streamlined enclosure and be waterproofto at least 5 meters of depth. In order to allow for smooth control, messages to the thrusters must be prioritized to avoid network conflicts and should only be received by the required device. To allow individual thruster control, the message frame is manipulated to allow node specific communication; this is then converted to Dshot protocol by the microprocessor to communicate with the ESC. Using a custom PCB with SMD allows the CAN bus electronics to fit in the required width and allow space for the microprocessor and ESC to fit inside the 3D printed enclosure, which is then filled with epoxy for waterproofing of the electronics.
