A migrant worker gets their passport confiscated by their employer.

No proof. No backup. No way to call for help without being caught. This happens to millions of people. 164 million migrant workers worldwide face exploitation, document theft, and blackmail, often with no safe way to store evidence or ask for help.

At Hack the Gap, Marie aimee Rabourdin, Romain Alvarez, Fabrice GUARRACI and Imane Rakraki built SafeKeeper: an offline-first app that lets migrant workers securely store their documents, capture tamper-proof evidence of abuse, and send emergency SOS alerts. Even in areas with almost no connectivity.

One button. Locks the app. Clears the cache. Sends your location to trusted contacts. 

In 6 days, they: 
→ Built a working Android app (their first time using Flutter) 
→ Got 42 downloads 
→ Collected feedback from 16 actual migrant workers and 2 NGOs (Emmaüs, France Terre d'Asile) 
→ Started conversations with the Red Cross for a pilot

The hackathon is over. They're not stopping.

If you work with migrant populations, refugee support, or labor rights, reach out to become a pilot partner!

I’ve spent quite some time investigating AI adoption in gaming, digging into the disconnect between studios facing internal and public backlash and the discreet, massive adoption of AI tools throughout their production pipelines.

👉 Link in the comment

The financial pressure combined with competition from Chinese studios that are totally uninhibited in adopting generative AI is making adoption existential for publishers.

So far, the industry has drawn a clear line where nothing player facing is AI generated, at least officially. The reality is likely more subtle.

I’ve spoken with studios of all sizes, from major publishers to small indies, and with founders building the next generation of tools and engines (Arcade AI, X&Immersion, Alakazam Studios, Scenario, Chat3D) for this new paradigm to emerge.

🪙 𝗠𝘆 𝟮 𝗰𝗲𝗻𝘁𝘀.
I don’t think AI will replace game designers because creating great games requires something AI fundamentally lacks: the human experience of play. Fear, joy, frustration, and the tactile feel of controllers are concepts that can only be described to an AI.

Like any disruptive innovation, generative AI is going to reshape all industries, gaming probably more than most, and will require artists, writers, and developers to adapt to this new reality. This is a huge opportunity to reshuffle the deck, empower small teams and young creators, and reveal more game designers than any previous generation of tools.

What a day. After our TechLunch with the Reg.exe community, I spent the evening with a few members of 🔥 Bonfire, our community of indie game developers.

It was sincerely the best discussion I’ve had in decades about game creation, touching everything at once: industry-wide AI disruption and the strong rejection, the new tools that will democratize creation (Arcade AI, Alakazam), and the creative processes of everyone there, from team using no AI at all to another relying almost entirely on AI-generated video to train world models as game engines, and every shade in between.

AI or not, we all spoke the same language. Game design is hard regardless of the tools you use. And no matter how precisely we describe what it feels like to play a video game, AI will never feel it the way humans do. It’s like explaining how a bat perceives the world through echolocation. You can understand the mechanism, but you will never come close to experiencing it.

For the same reason, I don’t see AI creating innovative and exceptional games without precise human creative input. But we’ll need exceptional curation to filter out the AI slop. We’re not replacing game designers anytime soon, but we are definitely reshaping how games get made.

I’ll soon publish a much longer piece on our SOTA Substack about AI’s impact on video game creation. Stay tuned.

And that's the wrap on Hack the Gap. What a journey!

Three months ago, we made a bet that went against every rule of organizing a hackathon.

Usually, you mix ideation and building. Friday night is for brainstorming; Weekend is for coding.

We decided to break that model. To completely separate Ideation from Execution.

Our thesis was simple: In the age of AI, "building" isn't the bottleneck anymore. Understanding the problem is.

So we changed the rules. Participants had to arrive with the "What" already solved, so they could spend 48 hours obsessing over the "How."
They had two options:
- Work on problems we spent documented and researched by experts 
- Bring their own challenge but it had to be researched and documented to the same rigorous standards

The result?
2 out of the 3 winning teams worked on custom challenges.

At first glance, you might think this disproved our "expert-led" model. But it actually validated our deeper theory:

The source of the idea doesn't matter. The depth of preparation does.

These teams won because they didn't waste Friday night debating features. They could spend the weekend building, deploying, and getting real users.

One team hit 40+ signups. Another secured NGO partnerships. A third is being tested in classrooms this week.

This confirms that when you treat a hackathon as a "Product Execution Sprint" rather than an "Ideation Jam," the quality of output skyrockets.

This was v1. We learned a lot. But the core philosophy holds.

A massive personal thank you to my co-conspirators Pierina Camarena and Cristina Fernandez Bornay for jumping off this cliff together. And to BLACKBOX.AI for empowering, supporting our builders and making all of this possible. 

And to all the mentors who gave so much of their time and energy to guide the teams all along the event, thank you doesn't even cover it. You were the heart and soul of the event. 

Follow Hack the Gap to get the official winners' announcement and all of the pictures from last night. They're coming soon!
