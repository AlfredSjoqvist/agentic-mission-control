Increase a gear ratio by 100x and the reflected inertia increases by 10,000x.

That single physics problem is why we can build robots that backflip — but still can't build ones that fold laundry.

I learned this at Carnegie Mellon University's Lab to Market event in San Francisco last week, hosted by the Swartz Center for Entrepreneurship.

The presentation that really stuck with me was from Quanting Xie, a PhD student at CMU's Robotics Institute and co-founder of Origami Robotics.

His talk was called "The Dexterity Deadlocks — And How We Broke Them by Fixing the Hardware."

His core argument: we've seen humanoid robots do impressive locomotion — walking, flipping, dancing — but we still haven't seen them do truly meaningful dexterous manipulation. Why? Because the hardware is blocking the software.

Here's the problem he laid out:

🔧 Deadlock 1: Motors are too small, gear ratios are too large. When you shrink actuators for a human-sized hand, torque drops cubically with volume. Engineers compensate with high gear ratios — but that introduces massive friction, backlash, and the reflected inertia problem I mentioned above. All of this destroys the ability to transfer policies from simulation to the real world.

🤖 Deadlock 2: The embodiment gap. Human hands have ~22 degrees of freedom. If you want a robot hand the same size as a human hand with current motors, you end up severely underactuated (~6 DOF). That means the robot hand doesn't look like, move like, or behave like a human hand — which makes it nearly impossible to learn from human demonstration data.

His solution? Fix it at the root — build better hardware.

His team designed a custom axial flux motor (as opposed to traditional radial flux) that is 1.6x more torque-dense than the best commercial motor available. This allowed them to dramatically reduce gear ratios, closing the sim-to-real gap.

They then built what they call the Origami Hand — an anatomically correct, 22 degree-of-freedom, quasi-direct-drive robot hand that is similar in size to a human hand. They validated it across 27 different grasp taxonomies and an 80-tool toolbox.

He closed with a quote from Alan Kay — "People who are really serious about software should make their own hardware" — and the robotics adaptation from Yuke Zhu: "People who are really serious about robot learning should make their own robot hardware."

I walked away thinking this is exactly the kind of deep, first-principles work that actually moves the needle in physical AI. Not just better models — better atoms.

The ROV STEM kit we’re developing bridges the gap between low-performance but affordable kits and high-performance but expensive ones for ROVs. This idea emerged during our 2024 competition in Kingsport, Tennessee. The MATE competition drew hundreds of teams, from universities to high schools. Fortunately, I had the chance to talk with other teams and gain insights into their robots.

Talking to those teams, I saw it clear as day: passionate kids from schools without much budget are held back, while teams with money just compete ahead no problem. On top of that, the learning curve is steep—figuring out electronics, waterproofing, and controls from scratch is no joke for newcomers. Among the many I interacted with, most relied on premade, expensive parts from sources like Blue Robotics—high quality and reliable, but the high cost didn’t justify purchasing them for us.

That’s why our team sourced most parts in-house, from designing our own PCBs and waterproof brushless motors to building our own grippers. All these skills I’ve gained are a privilege that shouldn’t go to waste. That’s why my mentor, Ryan Chan , hired Marcus Mak, Aiden Matthew, and me to intern at his company, MakerMods. We’re developing the ROV STEM kit to make underwater robotics accessible—not limited by high price tags, but enabling everyone to pursue robotics, compete in events like MATE ROV COMPETITION , or simply have fun with ROVs in lakes.… more
