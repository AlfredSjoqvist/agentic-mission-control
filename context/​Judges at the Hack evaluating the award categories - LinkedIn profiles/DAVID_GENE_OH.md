A lot of the early spatial breakthroughs didn’t come from boardrooms. They came from hackers grinding late at night, experimenting on ideas that made zero sense on paper but pushed the industry forward experimenting.

Stanford was hacking and innovating in VR with the Virtual Human Interaction Lab, 10 years before the Oculus debut.

That’s why hackathons still matter. More than ever.

You learn what developers can really build with your tech.
Not the slide-deck version. Not the “roadmap.”
But echos back what their generation expects from this tech.

StanfordXR Immerse the Bay wrapped Sunday. And I was thoroughly impressed with what students and working professionals built with SecureMR and WebSpatial products. 

The worst part of hackathons is deciding who would 'win', but as cliche as it sounds; all who hacked with us during the 36 hours sprint were all winners.

Today the web is trapped in a 2D plane.
But the spatial web won’t live in a flat browser.

It’ll live in your room.
Modular, interactive, alive.

Imagine pulling a pair of sneakers off your favorite shop’s site and holding them in your hands. Or learning astronomy not from a diagram, but from planets orbiting in your living room. That’s the web we’re heading into.

And here’s the cool part: web devs already have the skills. React, HTML, CSS. That’s enough to build the next wave of spatial apps — no game engine, no appstore.

We launched the Web to Spatial Program to help devs make that leap. AI lowers the barrier to code. Spatial raises the roof (I still say this) of what’s possible.

🚨 Deadline this Wednesday.
If you’re a web developer who wants to jump into spatial — or an XR dev with a web background — this is your chance.
