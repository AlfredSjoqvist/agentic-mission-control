About
I am the cofounder and CEO of Devol Robots, where we are rethinking how machines learn to act in the real world. While most AI systems imitate humans through data, we are teaching robots to learn directly from experience, to understand contact, force, and intention through real-world interaction.

I have come to believe that true intelligence emerges not from imitation but from experience. The same way we learn through touch, effort, and failure, machines too must live in the world to understand it. That is the path we are building at Devol.

At Devol, our goal is simple: to build robots that do not just see the world, but understand it through interaction. We are building the foundation for a new era of physical intelligence — one that bridges perception, action, and learning in the real world.

Let me rewrite my cofounder's post in plain English:

Most robot learning today treats everything as a flat vector.

Joint angles, end-effector poses, forces, torques — all flattened, concatenated, and fed into a neural network as if they lived in the same space. Sometimes they’re tokenized, sometimes embedded, but the assumption is the same: the network will figure it out.

That assumption is fundamentally wrong.

Different parts of a robot’s state live in fundamentally different spaces.
Pose is not force. Force is not configuration. Contact is not motion.
When we flatten them into one vector, we erase the structure that actually governs physical interaction.

As a result, the world model never truly “understands” interaction — it only fits curves.

If we want robots to manipulate the real world robustly, we need representations that respect the spaces these signals naturally live in. State and action should be encoded in ways that preserve their physical meaning, so that geometry, contact, and interaction are explicit in the model — not something the network has to rediscover from scratch.

Physics isn’t flat.
Our representations shouldn’t be either.

I was at an event featuring Ed Chi (VP Research, Google DeepMind), and one idea stayed with me after the talk ended:

Platform shifts aren’t about features. They’re about new underlying models of the world.

Ed walked through how the internet from 1995–2015 was built on three foundational ideas: indexing, vector representations, and ranking systems. That stack powered search and recommendation — and it still drives hundreds of billions of dollars today.

Then came the next shift: sequential models, reasoning steps (chain-of-thought), and post-training loops that dramatically improved reliability. That architectural change unlocked modern multimodal AI systems — the kind that can see, reason, and assist in real time.

As a founder building a world model for robotics manipulation at Devol, I couldn’t help but see the parallel.

Robotics today still largely runs on brittle pipelines:
Perception → Planning → Control
Handcrafted rules → Narrow tasks → Limited generalization

It works — but only within tight constraints.

The real breakthrough won’t be “a better controller” or “a better grasping algorithm.”
It will be a new underlying world model for physical interaction.

Instead of scripting reactions, the system predicts.
Instead of hardcoding contact logic, it reasons about force and state transitions.
Instead of solving one task, it understands structure across tasks.

In language models, reasoning improved when intermediate explanations were introduced.
In robotics, that equivalent is structured intermediate representations — object-centric reasoning, contact modeling, subgoal decomposition.

In AI, post-training loops turned base models into reliable assistants.
In robotics, tight real-world feedback loops will turn manipulation from demos into production-grade intelligence.

An assistant that can see and reason is powerful.
An assistant that can see, reason, and act in the physical world is transformative.

The internet is being reconfigured by multimodal reasoning systems.

The physical world will follow.

Just some of deep thoughts in the late nights......
"The Euclidean Inductive Bias is holding back Generalist Robot Policies"
We treat the State Space S and Action Space A of robots as flat Euclidean vectors. I think this is a fundamental category error.
Robotic manipulation doesn't happen in a vacuum; it happens on curved manifolds.
Interaction Dynamics - We treat robot data such as end-effector pose, joint angles, and force/torque profiles as generic vectors, but they inhabit fundamentally different spaces. Poses evolve on Lie Groups (SE(3)), while the stiffness and inertia matrices that govern joint torques and interaction forces live on the Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) cone. Linearly interpolating these matrices in Euclidean space violates the geometry of the manifold and leads to unstable interaction behaviors. Perhaps we need to compute geodesics using the Affine-Invariant Metric, not the Frobenius norm.
Hierarchical planning - Long horizon tasks naturally form a tree structure. Embedding these into n-dimensional Euclidean space causes massive distortion. We should be encoding high-level intent in Hyperbolic Space, where the exponential volume expansion naturally accommodates hierarchical complexity.
Dynamics Learning in World Model - Predicting next-state transitions in a world model ignores the underlying Lie Group structure of rigid body motion. SOTA World Models must evolve to learn Geodesic Flows on the manifold, ensuring that predictions remain physically consistent (diffeomorphic) by design.
At Devol, we are moving beyond "fitting curves" in flat space. We are exploring how Riemannian Geometry and Parallel Transport can serve as the mathematical backbone for a truly robust World Model. One that truly and ultimately respects the intrinsic curvature of contact and motion.
Physics is curved...
To truly solve manipulation, our representations must be too.… more

Here’s what we believe is true about robotics.

Dexterity doesn’t come from learning more actions.
It comes from understanding how the world responds to interaction.

The mainstream approach learns how humans move, not the underlying physics behind that movement. Scaling tele-operated data and vision-language-action models mostly scales imitation — including human fine corrections and jerkiness.

The better model is this:
Learn the physical intent behind motion — stiffness, compliance, and interaction outcome — not the trajectory itself.

Here’s how this shows up in practice:
When robots learn actions, they inherit human jitter. When they learn interaction and control intent, behavior becomes smooth, stable, and transferable — and improves with deployment instead of plateauing.

That’s the difference between copying motion and learning physical truth.… more
