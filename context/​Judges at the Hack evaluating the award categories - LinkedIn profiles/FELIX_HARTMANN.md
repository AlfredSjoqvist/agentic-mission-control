About
Felix Hartmann is the Founder and Managing Partner of Hartmann Capital, a frontier tech investment firm. The thesis of the permanent underclass has two core knock on effects that are largely under allocated. 

A free market economy is largely a competence hierarchy composed of individuals and their ability to generate value for others. Think of it as each human having a score that is some combination of ambition (hours worked) x genetic predisposition (ie IQ) x leverage (technology or resources). 

While neither IQ nor ambition scale, technology does. A 100 IQ working 30 hours will overtime beat a 110 IQ working 40 hours given that the 100 IQ uses technology in a way to either double his output, or make him produce the results of a 120 IQ. 

Now naturally in a free market economy, this experiment will lead to top percentile IQs that work every waking hour, trying to outcompete each other. 

The permanent underclass is the population that falls below the line where neither them without AI, nor them with AI can compete with a standalone agent. As of today, this line does not really exist yet, as virtually all AI products, still require a human manager. 

Thus the North Star for both those wishing to escape that ever rising base line and those wishing to compete at the very top will try to find ways to increase their score. 

Where can people get advantages from? 
Enhancing their own compute (brain power) both technologically (BCI/AR) and organically (biotech). Equally we may see physical power enhancements (mechanically and biologically), where the cost of enhancement is a fraction of a comparable humanoid. 

Now assuming all the above is true, two knock on effects become unavoidable. Enhancing yourself will be a core tenant of the upper class, while finding experiences to fill the void left by exiting the productive class will be a core tenant of the underclass.… more

Most breakout tech today sounded like sci-fi ten years ago. From humanoids, to LLMs to longevity it’s all happening.

So what then feels like sci-fi today that is ripe for breakthrough in the coming decade? One of those spaces we are focusing on is brain computer interfaces. From headphones that monitor your mood state like Neurable, to small devices that can “read your thoughts” by measuring the electromagnetic frequencies emitted by your muscles, all the way to full-on brain chips like Paradromics and Neuralink that connect directly to your neurons and can transfer information bit by bit from your brain to machines. 

While BCI seems far out, we believe it’s one of the technological endgames for computing and human machine interaction. Even the most invasive companies requiring brain surgery have entered human clinical trials and are going commercial by 2030.

Where you invest may be just as important as what you invest in. While the SF/Bay Area only represents ~2.25% of the US population, it generates ~42% of all US unicorn startups.

Naturally a debate emerges. If you have such a statistical edge investing in the honey pot of innovation, why would you touch any other geography/demographic when the hit rate appears to be much worse? 

Markets tend to trend towards efficiency though, so while the sum of winners is larger, so is the density of deals as more founders flock to SF than any other city. Equally, when there’s more bidders, something has to give and you end up paying a premium for founders just because they’re based in SF. 

To me the edge lies in just backing the best founders no matter where and being willing to pay market. That means in SF you will pay premium for a rockstar team. It also means you will be able to strike some incredible deals in overlooked places like Eastern Europe or Canada. And while the underdog may have a lower statistical hit rate, your pay-off may be 10x when you swap the premium for a discount. 

Best of both worlds? Truffle-hunt outlier founders in overlooked geographies, back them early, then help them relocate to the Bay Area.… more

One of the singular most important jobs technology can solve for right now is upgrading humans.

How can someone stay economically competitive in a world that is disrupted by AI but has not yet structurally reformed with UBI?

While we have little control over reform, we can always push the lever of increasing economic competitiveness.

Smart glasses to me are the lowest hanging fruit that will be adapted near term that allows humans to tap into the brain power of AI in all industries.

A store clerk can walk the aisles while his camera glasses scan the shelves and update inventory automatically, and the display keeps his next tasks lined up queued by the manager.

An oil rig worker has his AI copilot serve as a second layer of safety through computer vision, ensuring the wrong valve wasn’t left open by accident.

An entry level HVAC mechanic, can get in field guidance by AI or someone more senior through his POV viewpoint.

Humans still are much advanced to humanoids in dexterity, weight capacity, and edge case problem solving. Upgrade the human workforce with smart glasses and you not only see real GDP growth, but also ensure humans remain competitive against machines for another 10 years until BCI can carry on the torch of augmentation.… more

Sunk cost is one of the most common investing mistakes people make. 

In his book series Market Wizards, Jack Schwager interviewed some of the biggest investing legends in the world, from Druckenmiller and Soros to Dalio and Marks. 

One of the most frequently shared insights was that whether you acknowledge it or not, the choice to hold an asset is the same as choosing to buy it again. So if you would not re-underwrite an investment today at the current market price, you likely should re-evaluate that investment. 

This is particularly important in edge cases. If an asset underwent meaningful appreciation, you need to stress test whether the set up still beats its opportunity cost. On the flip side, if something declined in value, the market does not care whether you want to exit when it gets back to breakeven. 

The only thing you should measure against, is not your entry, but rather would you make this investment again today?… more

Cold email isn’t dead. If you do it right.

If you ask most VCs they have done little to no deals that came in cold. Whether you love it or hate it, the game favors social signaling.

That being said one of my most interesting deals actually came in cold last year. Can you make telepathy happen with BCI? Wild enough concept, and most wearables guys said “probably not legit”… in VC probably isn’t good enough so I booked a flight to NYC to check it out.

Seeing the lab and early demo first hand, and spending a day with the founder made it clear there was something real here, so we kept tracking the deal. After four months of status updates, a lead came in and within a week or two, the company had an all star round, that was virtually entirely sourced cold, and then oversubscribed to a higher valuation.

If you’re a VC: Stay curious

If you’re a founder: Don’t listen to the gatekeepers and shoot your shots.… more
