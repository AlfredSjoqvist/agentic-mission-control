# Wildfire Research: Full Lifecycle from Ignition to Suppression

Compiled for FireSight hackathon project — realistic simulation reference.

---

## 1. How Wildfires Start — Every Cause

### Human vs Natural Split
- **84-89% of US wildfires are human-caused** (PNAS 2017 study: 84%; 2018-2022 average: 89%)
- **Lightning causes ~10-16% of US wildfires** but accounts for **40% of fires and 69% of acres burned** in the western US
- Human activity is **7x more likely** to be the cause than lightning
- Human-caused fire season is **3x longer** than lightning-caused season
- Human fires add an average of **40,000 additional wildfires per year**
- Human fires dominate in **5.1 million km2** of the US; lightning fires dominate in only **0.7 million km2** (sparsely populated mountainous West)

### Lightning Ignition
- **Dry lightning**: Thunderstorm where precipitation evaporates before reaching ground. Threshold: <0.1 inches (2.5mm) of rain. Most dangerous because ground stays dry.
- **Wet lightning**: Can still start fires — research shows fires started despite up to 7.7mm (~0.3 inches) of precipitation
- **Long-continuing-current (LCC) lightning** is particularly significant for fire ignition — sustained current heats fuel longer
- When **live fuel moisture content (LFMC)** is dry, probability of fire per lightning strike is **2.25%** vs **1.27%** when wet — an **80% increase** in wildfire likelihood
- Lightning strikes the US ~25 million times/year

### Power Lines (Utility-Caused Fires)
Power lines start fires through several mechanisms:
1. **Arc faults (High-Impedance Faults/HiZ)**: When a conductor falls to earth, in ~30% of cases the fault draws too little current to blow a fuse. The line stays energized on/near ground for **tens of minutes**, producing high-energy, high-temperature arcing
2. **Vegetation contact**: Trees falling into lines, branches growing into conductors. Creates high-impedance fault-to-ground, potential flashover
3. **Conductor clashing**: High winds cause power lines to hit each other, throwing off **incandescent metal particles**. Aluminum conductors can burn as particles fall
4. **Equipment failure**: Aging hardware (hooks, insulators, transformers) failing and dropping hot metal or arcing

**Camp Fire (2018)**: A single worn metal hook on a PG&E transmission tower snapped. The power line struck the tower, producing electric arc and molten metal that ignited dry brush. **84 deaths**. PG&E pleaded guilty to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter.

**Dixie Fire (2021)**: A 65-foot Douglas fir fell onto a PG&E distribution line at 6:48 AM. Two fuses blew but one remained active. High voltage traveled down the tree trunk to earth via **high impedance arc fault** — this was the ignition source. Burned **963,309 acres** — largest single fire in California history.

Both fires ignited in Feather River canyon, ~5 miles apart.

### Arson
- **21% of human-caused wildfires** are arson
- Can account for **up to 70%+ of fires** in some jurisdictions
- Arson fires are often set during high-danger conditions for maximum damage

### Debris Burning
- **29% of human-caused wildfires** — the single largest human cause
- Escaped agricultural burns, yard waste burns, trash burning

### Equipment
- **11% of human-caused fires**
- Mowers, chainsaws, vehicles (catalytic converters), welding, grinding
- Hot metal fragments from blade-on-rock contact
- Overheated bearings, dragging chains/metal

### Campfires
- **5% of human-caused fires**
- Unattended or improperly extinguished

### Cigarettes
- Historically significant but **declined 90% since 1980** due to changes in cigarette design (fire-safe cigarettes mandated in many states)

### Gender Reveal Parties / Fireworks
- **El Dorado Fire (2020)**: Smoke bomb at gender reveal photoshoot ignited dry grass. Burned **22,744 acres**, killed 1 firefighter. Couple charged with involuntary manslaughter.
- **Sawmill Fire (2017)**: Off-duty Border Patrol agent shot rifle at explosive target for gender reveal. **47,000 acres** burned in Arizona's Santa Rita Mountains.
- Children playing with fireworks/matches: **5% of human-caused fires**

### Prescribed Burns That Escape
- USFS conducts ~4,500 prescribed fires/year; about **7 escape** — less than 1%
- Florida (2005-2010): 131,506 burn authorizations, **530 escaped (0.40%)**
- Oklahoma study: **3.6% probability** of an escaped fire, **16.2% probability** of a spotfire
- **Hermits Peak/Calf Canyon Fire (2022, New Mexico)**: Two USFS prescribed fires escaped and merged. **340,000+ acres**, destroyed 160+ homes and 900+ structures — largest and most destructive wildfire in New Mexico history

### Conditions That Make Ignition More Likely

**Red Flag Warnings**: Issued when extremely dangerous fire weather is expected — high winds + very low humidity + very dry fuels. "Extreme Red Flag Warning" = conditions as dangerous as any in recent memory.

**Santa Ana Winds (Southern California)**:
- Katabatic ("descending") winds born in high deserts/mountains
- Cold air at high elevation tumbles westward and downhill toward lower coastal pressure
- Heats up ~10degC for every kilometer of elevation drop
- By coast: hot, dry, powerful winds across landscape already desiccated by months of summer heat
- Peak danger: October-January

**Diablo Winds (Northern California)**:
- Same mechanism as Santa Ana but in NorCal
- Most dangerous in fall when vegetation is driest
- Relative humidity plunges, drying out vegetation
- Drove the 2017 Tubbs Fire and 2019 Kincade Fire

**Fuel Moisture**: Vegetation moisture content is the critical variable. When below critical flammability threshold, wildfire likelihood jumps 80%.

### Timeline: Ignition to Out-of-Control
- Dried grass to flame from cigarette: **under 60 seconds** in low humidity
- Full preheating-to-spreading sequence: **under 30 seconds** in ideal conditions
- Small spark to life-threatening inferno: **minutes, not hours**
- Growth phase: **1 minute to several hours** depending on fuel and wind
- Camp Fire (2018): Expanded at **~80 football fields per minute** at peak
- Tubbs Fire (2017): Advanced **more than a mile in 20 minutes**

## 2. Fire Behavior — How Fires Actually Spread

### The Fire Triangle
Three elements required for combustion:
1. **Heat** — raises material to ignition temperature
2. **Fuel** — combustible material
3. **Oxygen** — oxidizing agent (~21% of atmosphere)

Remove any one element and fire goes out. This is the basis of all suppression tactics.

### The Fire Behavior Triangle
Three factors determine how fire behaves in the landscape:
1. **Fuels** — type, moisture, arrangement, loading
2. **Weather** — wind, humidity, temperature, stability
3. **Topography** — slope, aspect, elevation, terrain features

### Terrain Effects

**Uphill vs Downhill**:
- Fire moves **much faster uphill** — flames preheat, dehydrate, and ignite fuels above
- Steeper slope = more preheating = faster fire
- Downhill spread is slower — less preheating of fuels below
- Rule of thumb: fire speed doubles for every 20% increase in slope

**Canyon/Chimney Effect**:
- Canyons and ravines funnel wind and fire like chimneys
- Creates strong upslope drafts → **extreme fire behavior**
- Box canyons are death traps — fire can accelerate unpredictably

**Aspect**:
- South-facing slopes: more direct sunlight, hotter, drier, higher fire severity
- North-facing slopes: cooler, moister, generally less severe

### Wind Effects

**Santa Ana / Diablo Winds**: Already covered above. Can push fires at 10-19 mph through populated areas.

**Fire Whirls / "Firenadoes"**:
- Form when superheated air rises rapidly and begins rotating
- Draw in more air and flames upward in tight spiral
- Most stay small (1-2 feet diameter)
- Some grow to **400 feet tall and 50 feet wide**
- Carr Fire (2018): Fire tornado with winds up to **143 mph**, responsible for 4 of 8 deaths
- Canberra bushfires (2003): Horizontal winds of **160 mph**, vertical air speed **93 mph**
- Can reach **EF-1 to EF-2** on Enhanced Fujita Scale

### Spot Fires and Ember Transport
- **Spot fires**: New fires started outside the main fire perimeter by airborne burning embers (firebrands)
- Embers lofted by fire plumes and transported by convection columns or wind
- **Transport distance**: Up to **20 km (12 miles)** ahead of fire front (documented in Australian bushfires)
- Spot fires are one of the most dangerous behaviors — they can cut off escape routes and overwhelm suppression efforts

### Fire Types

**Surface Fire**: Burns ground-level fuels (leaf litter, debris, grass, low shrubs). Relatively lower temperature (<400degC / 750degF). Most common fire type.

**Crown Fire**: Burns at canopy level — treetops, vines, mosses. Fast-moving, extreme intensity. Wind has larger impact on spread. Can travel through interconnected foliage at terrifying speed.

**Ground Fire**: Burns organic material **beneath** the surface — peat, coal seams, tree roots. Can smolder for weeks/months. Nearly invisible and extremely difficult to extinguish.

### Pyroconvection — Fire Creating Its Own Weather
- Large fires create massive convection columns of superheated air
- Can generate **pyrocumulonimbus (pyroCb) clouds** — fire-generated thunderstorms
- These storms produce lightning (which starts new fires), erratic winds, and sometimes fire tornadoes
- Downdrafts from pyroCbs can cause sudden, unpredictable wind shifts at ground level
- This is what killed the Granite Mountain Hotshots — a thunderstorm outflow boundary shifted the fire direction

### Wildfire Speeds
- **Forests**: Up to 10.8 km/h (6.7 mph)
- **Grasslands**: Up to 22 km/h (14 mph)
- **Chaparral/shrubs**: 5-10 mph with high flame lengths
- **Australian grassfires**: Documented exceeding **25 mph**
- **Black Saturday (Australia, 2009)**: 12-19 mph
- **Camp Fire (2018)**: ~80 football fields per minute at peak
- **Cedar Fire (2003)**: 29 miles in first 14 hours
- **Rule of thumb**: Rate of spread is ~10% of average wind speed

## 3. Initial Attack — First 10-60 Minutes

### Fire Discovery and Reporting
- Detection via: lookout towers, aerial patrol, satellite, 911 calls, smoke reports
- Report goes to **dispatch center** (local, regional, or interagency)

### Dispatch Protocol
- **Initial Attack Dispatcher** mobilizes preplanned response resources
- Resources dispatched based on: fire danger level, fuel type, values at risk, available resources
- After first unit arrives and sizes up, dispatcher redistributes assets to cover rest of the area

### Who Responds First
Closest available resources:
- **Engines** (Type 3-6 wildland engines, carrying 150-750 gallons)
- **Hand crews** (10-20 person crews with hand tools)
- **Helicopters** (water bucket drops, observation)
- **Air tankers** (retardant drops from fixed-wing aircraft)
- **Single resource personnel** (prevention patrol, lookouts)

### Initial Attack Doctrine
- **Anchor point**: First priority — find a safe spot (road, stream, burned area) to base operations where fire cannot flank you
- **Attack as directly as possible** — get close to fire edge, start building line from anchor point
- Size-up → resource order → begin suppression simultaneously
- IC reports conditions: fire size, rate of spread, fuel type, terrain, weather, values threatened

### The "10 AM Policy" (Historical)
- Established **1935** by US Forest Service after the catastrophic **1910 "Big Blowup"** (3 million acres burned in 2 days in MT/ID/WA)
- Decreed: **every fire must be suppressed by 10 AM the day following its report**
- Drove adoption of airplane surveillance, smokejumpers (1941), aggressive suppression culture
- Reinforced after **Tillamook Fire (1933)** — 300,000 acres in Oregon
- **Officially abandoned in 1978** — shift from total suppression to fire management
- Legacy: Created decades of fuel buildup that worsened modern fires

### Determining Containment vs Major Incident
- **>95% of unwanted wildland fires** are controlled during initial attack or extended attack
- Key factors: fire size and intensity at time of first resource arrival
- If fire exceeds initial attack capabilities → **escaped fire** → extended attack
- Agency administrator completes **Wildland Fire Situation Analysis (WFSA)** to evaluate alternatives

## 4. Extended Attack & Large Fire Management

### Transition from Initial Attack
- Fire has exceeded or is expected to exceed initial attack capabilities
- IC requests additional resources
- **Wildland Fire Decision Support System (WFDSS)** complexity analysis
- Transition is the most dangerous period — fatality data shows disproportionate deaths during transition

### Incident Management Team (IMT) Activation

**Type 5 Incident** (lowest complexity):
- Single resource or task force
- Few personnel
- Contained within a few hours

**Type 4 Incident**:
- Several single resources
- IC may be a local duty officer
- Contained within one operational period (shift)

**Type 3 Incident**:
- Multiple resources, may extend beyond one operational period
- Local/regional IMT activated
- IC3 (Incident Commander Type 3)

**Type 2 Incident**:
- Team of **20-35 people**
- Regional significance
- Operations section personnel approaching **200 per operational period**
- Total incident personnel approaching **500**

**Type 1 Incident** (highest complexity):
- Team of **35-50 people**
- National significance
- Operations section personnel may exceed **500 per operational period**
- Total incident personnel may exceed **1,000**

**Note**: As of 2024, NMAC transitioned all Type 1 and Type 2 IMTs to **Complex IMTs (CIMTs)**.

### Fire Camp — How It Works
- Can support **2,000+ personnel**
- A temporary city: food service, sleeping areas, sanitation, medical, equipment maintenance
- **Spike camps**: Satellite camps closer to the fire, draw supplies from main camp
- Key support roles:
  - Logistics/Facilities: Toilets, trash, generators, dust control, office space
  - Food Service: 3 hot meals/day for thousands
  - Medical: EMTs for heat illness, smoke inhalation, injuries
  - Transportation: CDL drivers moving equipment, fuel, water, supplies
  - Communications: Radio techs, IT support
  - Finance: Tracking costs (major fires cost $1M+/day)

### How Long Major Fires Burn
- Before 1986: Average **8 days**
- 2000s: Average **52 days**
- 2013: Average **37 days**
- Some fires: **weeks to months** (deep organic soils smolder for extended periods)
- Australian bushfires 2019-2020: Some lasted **5 months**
- Fire seasons themselves extended from ~4 months to **6-8 months**

## 5. Suppression Tactics — How They Actually Fight Fire

### Fireline Construction

**Hand Line**:
- Crew uses hand tools (Pulaski, McLeod, shovel, chainsaw) to scrape to **mineral soil**
- Width: 6 inches to 3 feet, guideline = **1.5x height of tallest dominant fuel**
- Width increases based on fuel types, wind, topography, spotting potential
- Rate: 20-person crew can build ~1 mile of line per hour in moderate fuels

**Dozer Line**:
- Bulldozers adapted for wildland use
- Three-dozer system: first walks down trees, second windrows them away, third cleans to mineral soil
- Results in fuel break **1-2 blade widths wide (~6 meters)**
- Best for large fires, moderate slopes, accessible terrain

**Wet Line**:
- Water from pump and hose to extinguish edge and wet adjacent fuels
- Temporary — must be followed up with constructed line
- Common with engine companies on accessible perimeter

### Direct vs Indirect Attack
- **Direct attack**: Working right at the fire's edge. Used when intensity allows safe approach.
- **Indirect attack**: Building line ahead of the fire, often using natural barriers. Used for high-intensity fires.

### Backfiring and Burnout Operations

**Burnout**: Uses fire to remove unburned fuel **between a control line and the fire's edge**. Small scale, routine.

**Backfire**: Large-scale firing operation to **change the direction or influence the fire's spread**. Set on the **downwind side** of a large fire, using the natural indraft to draw the backfire toward the main fire. Burns out fuel so the wildfire has nothing left to burn when it arrives.

- Both require careful timing, weather assessment, and experienced crews
- Use drip torches, fusees, or aerial ignition devices (ping-pong balls filled with potassium permanganate)
- One of the most effective but risky tactics

### Air Operations

**Air Tankers (Fixed Wing)**:
- Drop **fire retardant** (not water) ahead of fire's path
- Retardant composition: **85% water, 10% fertilizer/salt (ammonium phosphate), 5% color/thickener**
- Active ingredient: ammonium polyphosphate — chemical reaction with heat pulls heat out of fire
- Thickeners: guar gum and attapulgus clay prevent dispersal
- Red color aids aircrews in targeting
- Remains effective **for weeks/months** until washed away by heavy rain
- Large tankers carry up to **9,400 gallons**
- Dropped ahead of fire to create breaks, not directly on flames
- **Lead planes** fly low-level ahead of tankers to mark trajectory, ensure safety, direct the drop

**Helicopters**:
- Bambi buckets (500-2,600 gallons) or belly tanks
- More precise than fixed-wing — can work in tighter terrain
- Also used for: recon, crew transport, medical evacuation, aerial ignition

**Aerial restrictions**: Most aerial operations **halt at sunset** — poor visibility, risk of collision with terrain, towers, other aircraft. Low-altitude flights (100-120 feet) restricted to daytime.

### Hotshot Crews
- **20-25 elite wildland firefighters** per crew
- Primary tasks: constructing, firing out, and holding firebreaks using chainsaws, hand tools, ignition devices, water delivery
- Highly skilled in **complex burnout and backfire operations**
- Assigned to the **most challenging parts of the fire**
- Deploy for **14-21 consecutive days**, up to **16 hours/day**
- Line gear can weigh **65+ pounds**; daily hikes **4+ miles**; elevation gains **2,500+ vertical feet**
- Fitness requirement: Walk 3 miles with 45-pound pack in under 45 minutes
- 80 hours of critical training annually
- Can split into squads for parallel suppression efforts

### Equipment Types

**Engines**:
- Type 3 (heavy wildland): 500 gallons, pump, hose
- Type 6 (brush engine): 150-400 gallons, nimble, off-road capable
- Super Heavy: Up to **2,000 gallons**, extreme off-road capability

**Water Tenders**:
- Specialized vehicles bringing water to engines on the fireline
- Various sizes, key to sustaining fireline water supply

**Dozers**:
- Medium-sized best for all-around use — maneuverable on moderate slopes
- Heavy dozers for extreme fuel conditions
- Must have dozer boss (qualified supervisor)

### Night Operations
- Historically, fires "lay down" at night (cooler temps, higher humidity, less wind)
- Used for: mop-up, line improvement, burnout under calmer conditions
- **Dangers**: Loss of direction in dense forests, unseen hazards, fatigue
- Watch Out Situation #2: "In country not seen in daylight"
- Aerial support unavailable after sunset
- Increasingly, fires burn aggressively through the night due to climate change

### Structure Protection
- **Structure Protection Specialist (STPS)** leads operations
- Three phases: **Triage → Preparation → Defensible Tactical Action**
- Triage: Assess which structures are defensible vs impossible to save
- Preparation: Clear brush around structures, set up sprinklers, wrap structures in fire-resistant material
- Defense: Engine companies stand by to protect structures as fire passes
- Hard choices: Cannot protect everything — must prioritize based on defensibility and available resources

## 6. Containment & Mop-Up

### What "X% Contained" Actually Means
- Percentage of fire's **perimeter** secured by control lines
- 25% contained = control line around a quarter of the perimeter
- **NOT based on acres** burned or how much fire is still active
- Does not mean the fire is 25% "done" — active flames may still burn inside the perimeter

### How Containment Line is Established
- Combination of: hand line, dozer line, wet line, natural barriers (rivers, roads, rock)
- Must connect to form continuous barrier around entire perimeter
- Weakest point determines whether fire escapes

### Contained vs Controlled vs Out

**Contained**: Fire is encircled by control lines (constructed or natural fuel breaks). Reasonable expectation fire won't spread beyond them. **Active flames may still threaten to cross.**

**Controlled**: Fully contained AND crews have extinguished flames and smoking/smoldering fuels, removed unburnt fuels from about **300 feet inside the fire line perimeter**.

**Declared Out**: **No hotspots or smoke detected within lines for at least 48 hours.**

### Mop-Up Process
- Extinguishing or removing burning material near control lines
- Scraping down to **mineral soil**
- Felling fire-damaged trees (snags) that could fall across control lines
- Cooling ash pits
- **Cold-trailing**: Running the back of your hand along the ground near the fireline to detect residual heat
- Duration: Days for small fires, **weeks or months** for large fires with deep organic soils
- Most labor-intensive and tedious phase — but critical for preventing re-ignition

## 7. California-Specific

### CAL FIRE Structure
- **California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection**
- Fire department of the California Natural Resources Agency
- Responsible for fire protection on **31 million acres** of state and private wildlands
- Provides emergency services in **36 of 58 counties** via contracts with local governments

### Staffing
- ~**4,000 permanent** firefighters
- ~**2,500 seasonal** firefighters
- 72-hour workweek: three consecutive 24-hour days followed by four days off
- Four 72-hour workweeks in a 28-day cycle

### Facilities (530+ statewide)
- **234 fire stations**
- **112 telecommunications facilities**
- **31 conservation camps** (inmate fire crews)
- **8 fire crew camps**
- **21 unit headquarters**
- **13 air attack bases**
- **10 helitack bases**
- **3 training centers**
- 1 forestry nursery, 8 state forests

### Seasonal Staffing Adjustment
- **Peak season** (June-October): 356 engines, 12 air bases, 10 helitack bases
- **Base staffing** (December-March): 65 engines, **no aerial resources**
- Transitional periods scale up/down based on projected fire risk

### Budget
- 2020-21: **$2.5 billion** total ($2.1 billion for wildfire response)
- Includes base budget + Emergency Fund (E-Fund)

### Responsibility Areas

**SRA (State Responsibility Area)**: State is financially responsible for wildfire prevention/suppression. Does not include cities or federal land. CAL FIRE jurisdiction.

**FRA (Federal Responsibility Area)**: Federal government manages (USFS, BLM, NPS). US government-owned lands.

**LRA (Local Responsibility Area)**: Local fire departments and agencies. Incorporated cities and urban regions not in SRA or FRA.

### Mutual Aid System
- **California Master Mutual Aid Agreement**: CAL FIRE assists other departments when local resources depleted and CAL FIRE resources available
- When disaster overwhelms local capacity → request additional resources from **mutual aid region** → state system
- **CWCG (California Wildfire Coordinating Group)** manages Direct Protection Area agreements between agencies
- Federal, state, and local coordination through ICS (Incident Command System) — standardized management structure

## 8. The Human Side

### Danger Statistics
- **400+ on-duty wildland firefighting fatalities** in US from 2000-2019
- Wildland fatalities increased from **2% to 10%** of total firefighter fatalities (500% relative increase) between 2002-2012 and 2013-2022
- Burn-related fatalities increased from **9% to 27%** of deaths
- Vehicle accident fatalities increased **107%** (annual average)
- Hazards: biological, chemical, ergonomic, physical, psychosocial, and safety

### The 10 Standard Firefighting Orders
Developed 1957. Organized in deliberate sequence: fire behavior → fireline safety → organizational control.

**Fire Behavior (1-3):**
1. Keep informed on fire weather conditions and forecasts
2. Know what your fire is doing at all times
3. Base all actions on current and expected behavior of the fire

**Fireline Safety (4-6):**
4. Identify escape routes and safety zones and make them known
5. Post lookouts when there is possible danger
6. Be alert. Keep calm. Think clearly. Act decisively.

**Organizational Control (7-9):**
7. Maintain prompt communications with your forces, your supervisor, and adjoining forces
8. Give clear instructions and be sure they are understood
9. Maintain control of your forces at all times

**10. Fight fire aggressively, having provided for safety first.**
(Only after considering, discussing, and acting on the previous 9)

### The 18 Watch Out Situations
Originally 13 (1957), expanded to 18 in 1987.

1. Fire not scouted and sized up
2. In country not seen in daylight
3. Safety zones and escape routes not identified
4. Unfamiliar with weather and local factors influencing fire behavior
5. Uninformed on strategy, tactics, and hazards
6. Instructions and assignments not clear
7. No communication link with crew members or supervisor
8. Constructing line without safe anchor point
9. Building fireline downhill with fire below
10. Attempting frontal assault on fire
11. Unburned fuel between you and fire
12. Cannot see main fire; not in contact with someone who can
13. On a hillside where rolling material can ignite fuel below
14. Weather becoming hotter and drier
15. Wind increases and/or changes direction
16. Getting frequent spot fires across line
17. Terrain and fuels make escape to safety zones difficult
18. Taking a nap near fireline

### LCES (Lookouts, Communications, Escape Routes, Safety Zones)
Developed by **Paul Gleason** as a direct response to firefighter fatalities. Simplified safety system designed to be remembered under stress.

- **Lookouts**: Posted in potentially hazardous situations to monitor fire behavior, weather, other hazards
- **Communications**: Clear, concise, understood — face-to-face or radio
- **Escape Routes**: Pathways identified and communicated to all personnel. Minimum of **two escape routes** from work location to safety zone
- **Safety Zones**: Areas large enough to survive fire burning around you without other protection. Often "into the black" (already burned area)

Must be established **every time** a firefighter works near an objective hazard.

### Notable Firefighter Tragedies

**Mann Gulch Fire (August 5, 1949, Montana)**:
- 15 smokejumpers parachuted in + 1 ground firefighter = 16 total
- Fire spotted ahead of them, cutting off escape route to river
- Forced to turn uphill — fire outran them on dry grass, steep south-facing slope
- **13 killed** (11 in fire, 2 died of burns later)
- Only 3 survived — Foreman Wag Dodge lit an **escape fire** (first recorded use), 2 others reached ridge
- Led to revolution in fire behavior research and safety protocols

**South Canyon / Storm King Mountain Fire (July 6, 1994, Colorado)**:
- Lightning-started fire near Glenwood Springs, initially low priority
- Dry cold front moved through — sudden strong winds
- Spot fires made rapid uphill runs, entrapping multiple groups
- **14 killed**: 12 hotshots/smokejumpers on west flank, 2 helitack crew upslope
- Led to major safety reforms and reinvigoration of LCES training

**Yarnell Hill Fire (June 30, 2013, Arizona)**:
- Dry lightning ignition, June 28
- **19 of 20 Granite Mountain Hotshots killed** — sole survivor was lookout (Brendan McDonough) posted away from crew
- Thunderstorm outflow boundary shifted fire direction at ~4:30 PM — fire speed tripled, racing south at **10-12 mph**
- Crew had left safety zone "in the black" and moved into box canyon
- Had **less than 2 minutes** to attempt fire shelter deployment
- Deadliest US wildland fire for firefighters since **1933 Griffith Park Fire** (29 killed)
- Failures: ASFD didn't implement extended attack guidelines, risk assessments not completed, strategy failure not acknowledged

**Common thread**: ~80% of entrapment fatalities (1990-2013) occurred with firefighters **working upslope from a fire** when sudden upslope fire runs overtook them.
